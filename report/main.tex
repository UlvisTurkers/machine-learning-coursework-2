\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Deep learning models require large quantities of labeled data, yet in
many domains (medical imaging, autonomous driving, scientific discovery)
obtaining labels demands costly expert annotation.
\emph{Active learning}~(AL) addresses this by selecting which unlabeled
examples to annotate, reducing the budget needed to reach a target
accuracy~\cite{settles2009active}.

Most AL strategies rely on a trained model.  Uncertainty
sampling~\cite{lewis1994sequential} selects examples where the model is
least confident; BADGE~\cite{ash2020badge} uses gradient embeddings;
CoreSet~\cite{sener2018active} maximises coverage.  However, these face
a \emph{cold-start problem}: with $\leq$50 labels the model is too weak
for meaningful confidence estimates, so model-dependent strategies
degrade to random performance~\cite{hacohen2022active}.

Hacohen et~al.~\cite{hacohen2022active} resolve this with
\typiclust{}, a \emph{model-free} strategy.  Self-supervised features
(\simclr{}~\cite{chen2020simclr}) are learned without labels; the space
is clustered, and the most \emph{typical} (highest density) point per
uncovered cluster is selected.  Grounded in phase-transition theory,
\typiclust{} outperforms all eight baselines at low budgets.

We reproduce \typiclust{} on CIFAR-10 under two evaluation frameworks,
compare against random and published baselines, and propose a cosine
typicality modification for L2-normalised feature spaces.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

The \tpcrp{} variant operates in three stages.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stage 1: Representation Learning.}\quad
A \simclr{} encoder (ResNet-18~\cite{he2016resnet}, 3$\times$3 stem, no
max-pool) is trained on the full unlabeled pool with NT-Xent loss
($\tau{=}0.5$, SGD, lr$=$0.4, cosine schedule, 500 epochs, batch 512).
The 512-dim features are L2-normalised and cached.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stage 2: Clustering.}\quad
At each round with labeled set $\mathcal{L}$ and budget $B$,
$K{=}\min(|\mathcal{L}|{+}B,500)$ clusters are computed via $K$-Means.
Clusters with labeled samples are \emph{covered}; the rest are sorted
by size, ensuring diversity.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stage 3: Typicality Selection.}\quad
From each of the $B$ largest uncovered clusters we select the point with
highest typicality (local density via within-cluster KNN):
%
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Typ}(x)
  = \Bigl(\frac{1}{K_{\mathrm{nn}}}
    {\textstyle\sum_{x_i \in \mathrm{KNN}(x)}} \|x - x_i\|_2
    \Bigr)^{\!-1}
  \label{eq:typicality}
\end{equation}
%
with $K_{\mathrm{nn}}{=}\min(20,|\text{cluster}|)$.  Clusters with
$<$5 members are skipped.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\small
\DontPrintSemicolon
\SetKwInOut{Input}{Input}\SetKwInOut{Output}{Output}
\Input{Features $\mathbf{F}$, labeled set $\mathcal{L}$, budget $B$}
\Output{$B$ indices to label}
$K \leftarrow \min(|\mathcal{L}|{+}B,\;500)$\;
$\{C_1,\dots,C_K\} \leftarrow \text{KMeans}(\mathbf{F}, K)$\;
$\mathcal{U} \leftarrow \{C_i : C_i \cap \mathcal{L} = \emptyset\}$
  \tcp*{uncovered}
Sort $\mathcal{U}$ by $|C_i|$ descending\;
$S \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
\For{$C_i \in \mathcal{U}$ \textbf{while} $|S| < B$}{
  \lIf{$|C_i| < 5$}{\textbf{skip}}
  $S \leftarrow S \cup \{\arg\max_{x \in C_i} \operatorname{Typ}(x)\}$\;
}
\Return $S$\;
\caption{\typiclust{} Selection (\tpcrp{})}
\label{alg:typiclust}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent\textbf{Phase transition.}\quad
The authors identify a budget threshold below which model-free selection
dominates and above which uncertainty is superior.  \typiclust{} targets
the low-budget regime.

\noindent\textbf{Evaluation frameworks.}\quad
The paper uses three protocols: (i)~fully supervised (ResNet-18 from
scratch), (ii)~linear classifier on frozen \simclr{} features, and
(iii)~semi-supervised (FlexMatch).  We reproduce (i) and (ii);
(iii) is omitted due to computational constraints.

\section{Proposed Modification}
\label{sec:modification}

Since \simclr{} features are L2-normalised and lie on a unit
hypersphere~\cite{wang2020understanding}, we replace Euclidean
typicality with a cosine variant:
%
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Typ}_{\cos}(x)
  = \tfrac{1}{K_{\mathrm{nn}}}
    {\textstyle\sum_{x_i \in \mathrm{KNN}(x)}}
    \tfrac{x \cdot x_i}{\|x\|\|x_i\|}
  \label{eq:cosine-typ}
\end{equation}
%
On the unit sphere $\|a{-}b\|^2 = 2(1{-}\cos\theta)$, so the metrics
are monotonically related.  However, Euclidean typicality uses
$1/\text{mean}(d_i)$ where one large distance dominates the inverse,
while cosine uses $\text{mean}(s_i)$ where neighbours contribute
equally, offering more robustness to outliers.

We evaluate under both frameworks using the same features, seeds, and
budgets (Table~\ref{tab:mod}).  Both variants outperform random,
confirming the typicality principle is robust to the distance metric.
The modification adds negligible computational overhead.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We reproduced \typiclust{} on CIFAR-10 under two frameworks and
confirmed that typicality-based selection outperforms random sampling at
low budgets.  Published baselines confirm all model-dependent methods
perform at or below random in this regime.  Our cosine modification
shows the typicality principle is robust to the distance metric,
suggesting clustering and diversity drive \typiclust{}'s success.
